Week V

I learned how to give a proper and effective presentation. Through this, I understood:

  • The purpose of a presentation — to inform, influence, motivate, or educate an audience.
  • Presentations have two objectives:
    • Primary objective: The main goal of the presentation.
    • Secondary objectives: To engage, inspire, or support the audience’s understanding.
  • The content and tone of the presentation should match the audience (e.g., formal for business, casual for students).
  • How to design an effective PowerPoint presentation:
    • Use clear and simple slides.
    • Maintain consistent formatting.
    • Use Arial or Calibri font; size 14 for headings, 12 for content.
    • Limit to three colors for a professional appearance.
    • Avoid underlining text and use sentence case.
    • Always proofread the slides before presenting.
  • Presentation delivery includes verbal and non-verbal components:
    • Verbal: Clarity of speech, appropriate tone, and vocabulary.
    • Non-verbal: Eye contact, gestures, facial expressions, and posture.
  • Using cue cards can help stay on track during the presentation.

Week VI

In Week Six, I learned about public speaking, its objectives, different forms, and the importance of developing this skill. Public speaking helps individuals express their thoughts confidently, influence others, and communicate ideas effectively in various situations, such as presentations, debates, or discussions. I also explored different types of public speaking topics and the strategies to handle each one efficiently.

  • Abstract topics – Focus on ideas or concepts that don’t have a physical form, such as “freedom” or “happiness.”
  • Case-based topics – Involve analyzing a specific situation or event and presenting logical viewpoints about it.
  • Factual or scientific topics – Require the speaker to rely on data, evidence, and proven facts to support their statements.
  • Prescription-based topics – Focus on providing suggestions or solutions to address a problem.
  • Informational topics – Aim to educate or inform the audience about something new.
  • “What if” topics – Encourage creative and hypothetical thinking by imagining alternative scenarios or possibilities.
  • Problem-solution-based topics – Identify a specific problem and propose practical and effective solutions to resolve it.

Overall, learning about these topic types and strategies has improved my understanding of how to prepare and deliver effective speeches depending on the purpose and audience.
